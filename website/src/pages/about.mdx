## A quick history of AMP

AMP — originally **Accelerated Mobile Pages** — was announced by Google in 2015 in response to Facebook's Instant Articles and Apple News. Initially focused on speeding up mobile pages, AMP has evolved into a broader initiative to enhance user experience and content speed across various platforms. It sounded like a well-intended effort on first glance, but the results have been mixed at best.

For five years, Google Search's **Top Stories carousel** — located prominently above all other results — exclusively featured AMP pages on mobile devices. That placement generated a significant share of mobile-news clicks. As a result, many publishers felt compelled to adopt AMP, only to be surprised by a decline in their advertising revenue.

In July 2021, after public and legal pressure, Google dropped the AMP-exclusive requirement. But the damage was done. As Barry Adams put it:

> There was no other reason for Google to stop ranking these publishers in their mobile Top Stories carousel… Hundreds — if not thousands — of publishers ticked every single ranking box Google demanded: quality news content, easily crawlable and indexable technology stack, good editorial authority signals. But they didn't use AMP. So Google didn't rank them.
>
> Think for a moment about the cost of that. How many visits these publishers didn't get, simply because they didn't accept Google's blackmail. How much revenue was lost. How many jobs were affected. The ones that didn't survive.
>
> Just because Google demanded we embrace their pet AMP project.

## "Open source" isn't a defense

Despite AMP's assimilation into the OpenJS Foundation in 2019, many skeptics regard the move as superficial — and those suspicions have proven justified.

- Web standards advocate **Jeremy Keith** resigned from the AMP Advisory Committee in August 2021, noting that "it has become clear to me that AMP remains a Google product."
- Nine out of the top ten contributors to the AMP project on GitHub are Google employees.
- **Ferdy Christant** put it bluntly: *"AMP being open source isn't just a weak defense, it's no defense at all. I can open source a plan for genocide. The term 'open source' is meaningless if the thing that is open source is harmful."*

## Design decisions that lock you in

When a user navigates to a cached AMP page — via Google Search or a shared link — they unwittingly stay inside Google's ecosystem, since the original publisher's domain is obscured by the `google.com/amp` prefix.

To address this, Google introduced **Signed HTTP Exchanges**, a web standard letting browsers display the original site's URL instead of the prefixed one. But this technique obscures the fact that the page is still delivered by Google. Both Mozilla and Apple have criticized it as a harmful web standard. In contrast, Google's own browser supports it.

A familiar pattern: Google takes the actions that serve its interests, irrespective of external opinion. And of course, the data flows back. From Google's own support article: *"When you use the Google AMP Viewer, Google and the publisher that made the AMP page may each collect data about you."*

## "AMP makes the web faster"… does it?

The primary performance boost attributed to AMP doesn't actually originate from the framework — it comes from **preloading**. Preloading isn't unique to AMP; it just isn't widely available outside it. For uncached AMP pages, the speed improvements turn out to be minimal, if any.

A multi-state US antitrust case found that:

> After crippling AMP's compatibility with header bidding, Google went to market falsely telling publishers that adopting AMP would enhance page load times. But Google employees knew that AMP only improves the "median of performance" and "AMP pages can actually load slower than other publisher speed optimization techniques."

Internally, Google employees grappled with "how to publicly justify making something slower."

Terence Eden — another ex-committee member — resigned in December 2020:

> I remain convinced that AMP is poorly implemented, hostile to the interests of both users and publishers, and a proprietary and unnecessary incursion into the open web.

## Where this leaves us

AMP is on the decline. Publishers are moving away, usage is falling, and the legal pressure is mounting. The AMP team may have the best intentions, but the flaws and the harm to privacy and the Open Web cannot be ignored. As long as those issues persist, **u/AmputatorBot will be here, working to remove AMP from your URLs.**
